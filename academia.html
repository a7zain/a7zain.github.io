About Me

I have always been an observer. As a kid it was astrophotography — staying up to photograph a sky most people slept through, never satisfied with the picture until I understood the thing inside it. Engineering turned out to be that same instinct with better tools, and every step since has, one way or another, been about finding a sharper way to see.

I studied electrical engineering in Colorado because I wanted the physics behind what I'd been pointing a camera at, and I came home — first to Riyadh, then back to the Eastern Province — to put it to work where it matters to me. Engineering never let me stay in one lane, and I never wanted it to: I've worked on power systems and renewable-energy design, on the field side of oil and gas, and on satellite hardware — the requirements, the reviews, the qualification campaign that takes a design from a drawing to something proven fit to fly. At KACST and the Saudi Space Agency I learned what it actually takes to build for space, and that the discipline of it suits me.

The turn I didn't expect came from looking the other way. I'd always thought of myself as an observer of nature; space engineering showed me the most powerful nature-observation instruments ever built are already in orbit, pointed back at us. The same fleet I'd been treating as hardware was quietly recording the planet — the seasonal rise and fall of vegetation, water vapor moving across the peninsula, the routes birds repeat year after year without fail. Earth observation is where my curiosity sits now, and I have far more questions than answers, which is exactly how I prefer to start.

So I've built deliberately toward both ends of the same work: the hardware that goes up and the data that comes back down. What draws me is the seam where one system hands off to another — where what we build meets what we can actually measure — and I came home to do that here, in a space sector still deciding what it wants to be. I want to be part of deciding.
