Upstream

Building what goes up

Riyadh · August 2025 – January 2026 · King Abdulaziz City for Science and Technology · Saudi Space Agency · Exobotics

Where it started

As Systems Engineer and RAIL Lead on a six-person CU Boulder capstone team, I helped turn the Polarimetric Scanning Radiometer into a mobile platform that drives to field sites and measures radio-frequency interference bleeding into the protected 23.6–24 GHz band — the band weather satellites depend on to read water vapor. As Systems Engineer I owned the seams: the interfaces binding the instrument, towing vehicle, power, timing, and networking into one working system. As RAIL Lead I tracked every engineer's tasks against their subsystem, keeping four disciplines and six people moving in step. We disciplined three drifting onboard clocks to a microsecond GPS reference so every brightness reading landed at the right pointing angle; we found our filter too soft to guarantee it stayed inside the protected band, measured the error honestly at 40 K against a half-Kelvin target, and specified the hyperspectral path forward. It was passive remote sensing — data integrity for Earth observation — before I knew that was the direction I'd take.

The Saudi space program

Before I joined it, I studied where the Saudi program came from. It runs from amateur-radio microsatellites in 2000 to hyperspectral Earth observation to a cislunar science mission — and it crossed three launch powers to get there: Russian Dnepr rockets, a Chinese Long March, and finally an American Artemis flight. The cooperation with NASA on SaudiSat-4 in 2014 was the first thread of a relationship that SHMS, the mission I helped qualify, completes.

How I earned the seat

The path ran in three steps, and I climbed each one. The radiometer opened the door. The cooperative program — three months inside KACST — was where I proved I could work across a real satellite organization. And selection onto the Saudi Space Agency's Space Technologies Training Program, as the sole trainee carried over from KACST, was where I earned hands on the hardware itself. Nothing here was handed to me; each phase was the argument for the next.

Across the satellite lifecycle

I deliberately sought exposure across the full arc of a satellite program rather than depth in a single box. What follows maps where I worked, and how deeply.

Lifecycle ribbon (pass 3)

Mission analysis. During the cooperative phase I produced a series of mission-analysis studies, working in GMAT, STK, MATLAB, and Copernicus. I modelled a trans-lunar transfer trajectory in GMAT; ran an Earth-observation coverage study over Riyadh; and built a constellation trade study for national coverage, comparing three-, six-, and twelve-satellite architectures against revisit time, coverage fraction, orbital inclination, and altitude to find the point where added satellites stopped buying meaningful revisit. I analyzed ground-station access windows, and developed a communications link budget for a CubeSat downlink across UHF, S-band, and X-band — solving for pass duration, achievable data rate, daily imagery volume, and the best ground-station siting. These were the analyses that taught me to think about a spacecraft as a system of competing budgets before it is ever bent metal.

Systems engineering and subsystems. I rotated through the mission teams in two-to-four-week blocks, working alongside engineers in systems, attitude determination and control, electrical power, and communications. In the systems group I worked on requirements and interface definitions, contributed to trade studies, and sat in on design-review gates. In the subsystem rotations I went concrete: sensors and actuators on the ADCS side, battery and solar power budgeting on EPS, and RF band selection and ground-link planning on communications. The point was fluency — to be able to speak each subsystem's language at the interfaces where they meet.

Assembly, integration, and qualification. This is where the training became hands-on in a way analysis never is. Over roughly two months I took part in the assembly, integration, and test campaign for the satellite, working across subsystems rather than within one — harnessing, functional testing, and ADCS work among them — and through the environmental qualification campaign, including thermal and vibration testing. The work ran in an ISO Class 7 clean room, and it began with a week of clean-room and laboratory training: ESD discipline, full gowning, foreign-object-debris control, airborne-particle and temperature-and-humidity monitoring, strict material control, and documented IPA and lint-free cleaning against inspection logs. I worked with mechanical and electrical ground support equipment and ran functional and RF tests on Rohde & Schwarz instrumentation, executed test procedures, and logged anomalies — all to NASA standards. Watching a qualification campaign turn a stack of subsystems into flight-representative hardware is the part of this field you cannot learn from a textbook.

Mission analysis

I reproduce and extend these studies independently in Python — originally explored in STK and GMAT — to show the work, not just name the tools.

How many satellites does frequent revisit over Saudi Arabia actually take?

I modelled Walker constellations over Riyadh — circular 600 km orbits at 55° inclination, a 30° sensor reach, propagated across three days — and swept the satellite count. Going from three to six satellites collapses the worst-case revisit gap from a full day to under eight hours. But past six, adding satellites within the same three planes keeps improving mean revisit while the worst-case gap stalls near six and a half hours. The worst case isn't set by how many satellites you fly — it's set by how you arrange them: redistributing the same twelve satellites from three planes to four nearly halves the worst-case gap. Architecture beats headcount. (Coverage of a single point target sits under one percent, which is expected for narrow-cone point-target imaging — the meaningful metric is the revisit gap, not coverage fraction.)

Can one CubeSat send home everything it sees?

I built a three-band downlink budget for a 600 km CubeSat to a Riyadh ground station — free-space loss at the worst-case slant range, realistic antennas and noise figures, a 3 dB margin. Every band closes the link; that isn't the hard part. What separates them is bandwidth, and that's why a CubeSat flies three radios: UHF for telemetry, S-band for commands, and X-band — the only pipe wide enough at about 90 Mbps — for imagery. Resolving the link across a full pass, I let the modulation adapt from QPSK at the horizon to 16APSK near zenith, carrying 80 Mbps low and 133 Mbps overhead. Then I balanced that against the camera: a single Riyadh station supports roughly 34 minutes of imaging a day. For watching Saudi Arabia that's comfortable headroom — the link is oversized and revisit is the real constraint. Push the same satellite to commercial global tasking and one station can't keep up; you need four, or onboard storage and a downlink schedule. The radio was never the hard part. Knowing which constraint actually binds — that's the engineering.

How do you reach the Moon's neighbourhood, and what does speed cost?

I modelled a trans-lunar injection with patched conics — the first-order method you reach for before any high-fidelity tool. From a 600 km parking orbit, the injection burn is about 3.0 km/s and the coast out to lunar distance takes roughly five days. Then I swept the trade: you can cut the trip to two days, but the ΔV climbs sharply for the privilege, so the slow Hohmann-type transfer is the economical one. One detail stuck with me — you reach lunar distance crawling at 0.2 km/s while the Moon races past at 1.0, so arriving and staying are entirely different problems. It's a two-body approximation, not a flight trajectory — but it's the analysis that builds the intuition, and the satellite I helped qualify went on to fly cislunar for real.

SHMS

The satellite was SHMS (شمس, "the Sun") — a 12U CubeSat and Saudi Arabia's first dedicated space-weather mission, developed by Saudi national talent under the National Industrial Development and Logistics Program as part of a deliberate effort to localize space technology and put young Saudi engineers onto real flight hardware.

Its science is the Sun's reach into the space environment. From a highly elliptical orbit that carries it far beyond the shelter of Earth's magnetic field, SHMS studies four things — space radiation, solar X-rays, Earth's magnetic field, and solar energetic particles — the four domains the mission patch marks with four stars. That data protects the systems space weather quietly threatens: communications, aviation, navigation.

I worked the qualification campaign for SHMS at KACST — the test program that proves a design is fit to fly. Over roughly two months I took part in its assembly, integration, and environmental qualification across KACST's clean rooms and test facilities: thermal-vacuum, vibration, and electromagnetic compatibility.

I worked the qualification campaign for the design that flew.

On 4 April 2026, SHMS launched aboard NASA's Space Launch System on Artemis II — the most powerful rocket ever flown, and the first crewed voyage around the Moon in more than fifty years. It rode inside the Orion Stage Adapter, the ring beneath the Orion spacecraft that carries secondary payloads and releases them after separation, and deployed from there into space. With it, Saudi Arabia became the first Arab nation to fly a mission under the Artemis program. In NASA's records the satellite carries the designation SWC-1.

I had spent the autumn qualifying the design. Watching it leave the pad was the payoff.

Research and initiative

Alongside the hands-on work I produced a research report tracing the Kingdom's satellite lineage and the launch campaigns behind it — studying the vehicles at the level of staging, propulsion, and payload accommodation, and the launch sites across Chinese, Russian, and American providers. The arc ran from the SaudiSat-1 microsatellites that flew on converted Dnepr launchers out of Baikonur beginning in 2000 to the KACST-built SaudiSat-5A and 5B Earth-observation satellites that reached sun-synchronous orbit on a Long March 2D from Jiuquan in December 2018. My mentor read and reviewed the report. I also took the initiative to reach out to international instrumentation and component suppliers to obtain product specifications for testing and research — procurement-adjacent technical-liaison work I sought out rather than waiting to be assigned.

Recognition and what it opened

I completed the program with a certificate from the Space Technologies Training Program, and with something more durable: a place inside a small, fast-moving national community. My mentor, Abdulmajeed Alsharari, the ADCS Team Lead, was central to the experience and wrote my recommendation. The relationship outlasted the program — I now hold an ongoing research collaboration with KACST in Earth observation over sites in the Riyadh area, which is where this story crosses into the downstream work that occupies me now. The Saudi space sector is small enough that one good season inside it introduces you to nearly everyone building it; this was mine.
